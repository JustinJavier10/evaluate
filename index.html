Task 5: Short Answer Questions

1. Explain the relationship between data, information and knowledge

Data consists of raw facts and figures without context (e.g., "25", "Sydney", "Monday"). Information is data that has been processed, organized, and given context to make it meaningful (e.g., "25 books sold in Sydney on Monday"). Knowledge is information that has been analyzed, understood, and applied based on experience and insight (e.g., "Monday book sales in Sydney are typically 25% higher than other days, so we should increase staff on Mondays").

The relationship flows: Data → Information → Knowledge. Each level adds value through processing, context, and understanding.

2. What is quantitative data and how can you use it?

Quantitative data is numerical data that can be measured and expressed as numbers. It answers questions like "how many?", "how much?", or "how often?"

Uses:

  • Statistical analysis and trend identification
  • Creating charts, graphs, and visualizations
  • Making data-driven decisions based on metrics
  • Comparing performance across time periods
  • Example: Number of support tickets (50 per week), system uptime percentage (99.5%), response time (15 minutes average)

3. What is qualitative data and how can you use it?

Qualitative data is descriptive data that captures qualities, characteristics, and subjective information. It answers questions like "why?", "how?", or describes experiences and opinions.

Uses:

  • Understanding customer satisfaction and experiences
  • Identifying themes and patterns in feedback
  • Gaining insights into user behavior and preferences
  • Improving service quality based on detailed feedback
  • Example: Customer comments ("The support agent was very helpful"), interview responses, observation notes

4. Give an example of how quantitative and qualitative data can be used together

Example: IT Support Service Evaluation

Quantitative data: Survey shows 85% customer satisfaction rate, average response time is 12 minutes, 40 tickets resolved per day.

Qualitative data: Customer comments reveal: "Response was quick but the solution wasn't explained clearly," "Agent was friendly but didn't follow up," "Problem was fixed but I'd like more documentation."

Combined use: The high satisfaction numbers (quantitative) look good, but the qualitative feedback reveals specific areas for improvement (communication, follow-up, documentation). Together, they provide a complete picture: the service is fast and effective (numbers) but needs better communication and documentation (feedback). This leads to actionable improvements: implement post-resolution explanations and provide written documentation for all fixes.

5. What methods could you use to determine client requirements for a website design and key information sources?

Methods to determine client requirements:

  • Interviews: One-on-one discussions with stakeholders to understand needs and expectations
  • Questionnaires/Surveys: Structured forms to gather requirements from multiple users
  • Focus Groups: Group discussions with target users to gather diverse perspectives
  • Observation: Watching how users interact with current systems
  • Document Analysis: Reviewing existing business documents, competitor websites, industry standards
  • Prototyping: Creating mockups to gather feedback on design concepts
  • Workshops: Collaborative sessions with stakeholders to define requirements

Key information sources: Business owner, marketing team, sales staff, customers, website analytics, competitor analysis, industry best practices, accessibility guidelines

6. Give some examples of client requirements for a website design

  • Aesthetics: Looks professional and modern, reflects brand identity
  • Availability: 24/7 accessibility, reliable uptime
  • Usability: Easy to navigate, intuitive interface, mobile-responsive
  • Content Management: Easy to update content without technical knowledge
  • Performance: Fast loading times (under 3 seconds)
  • Functionality: Shopping cart, search feature, contact forms, product catalogue
  • Security: SSL certificate, secure payment processing, data protection
  • SEO: Search engine optimization for visibility
  • Maintenance: Regular updates included, affordable upgrade costs
  • Scalability: Ability to add features as business grows
  • Accessibility: Meets WCAG standards for users with disabilities

Task 6: Multiple Choice Questions

Summary of Key Concepts from Multiple Choice Questions:

Data Collection Methods

  • Primary methods: Questionnaires, Interviews, Observations, Focus Groups, Experiments
  • Secondary data: Previously collected information from official documents, personal documents, archived research
  • Rating scales: Typically 4-11 points for effective measurement

Questionnaire Design Principles

  • Avoid double-barrelled questions (asking two things at once)
  • Avoid double negatives and leading questions
  • Use multiple items to measure abstract constructs
  • Ensure response categories are mutually exclusive and exhaustive
  • Use natural language and understand your participants
  • Pilot test your questionnaire before full deployment

Types of Questions

  • Open-ended questions: Provide qualitative data in participants' own words
  • Closed-ended questions: Provide quantitative data based on predetermined categories
  • Contingency questions: Direct participants to different follow-up questions based on responses
  • Probes: Follow-up questions like "Why do you feel that way?" or "Anything else?"

Interview Types

  • Interview guide approach: Topics decided in advance but sequence/wording flexible
  • Informal conversational: Questions emerge from immediate context
  • Standardized open-ended: Exact wording and sequence predetermined

Observation Methods

  • Naturalistic observation: Used for exploratory qualitative research
  • Often cannot determine exactly why people behave as they do
  • Complete participant: Researcher is secretly a member of the group being studied

Service Level Agreements (SLA)

  • Goal: Maintain and improve IT service quality in line with business requirements
  • Customer-based SLA: Individual SLA with each customer group covering all services they use
  • SLA examples: Maximum response time, minimum up-time, types of information provided
  • Implementation process: Catalogue services → Establish SLRs → Review contracts/OLAs → Negotiate → Agree SLAs
  • Formality: Organizations use Service Level Agreements to formalize internal customer relationships

Key Measurements

  • Likert Scale: Also called summated rating scale, typically 5-7 points
  • Mixed methods: Combine methods for complementary strengths and non-overlapping weaknesses (fundamental principle of mixed research)
